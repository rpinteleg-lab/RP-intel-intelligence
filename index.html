Summary
›
Executive Summary
Maersk and Hapag-Lloyd are rerouting their joint AE15 Asia-Mediterranean-Europe service through the Suez Canal, linking ports in China, South Korea, Malaysia, Egypt, Sri Lanka, and Singapore. The move follows a security assessment of the Red Sea and is conditional on continued stability in the region. The Suez Canal Authority reported a 23% year-over-year increase in revenues to $4.8 billion in FY 2025/26, with transits rising 10%. However, the recovery is reversible, and contingency plans are in place in case the situation deteriorates. The return of the service to the Suez Canal puts Egyptian ports back on a direct Asia-Europe loop for the first time since the outbreak of the US-Israeli war on Iran. The Suez Canal Authority aims to reach $8 billion in revenues by the second half of 2027.

The Egyptian government has finalized the second iteration of its State Ownership Policy, updating the 2022 framework to adopt a company-by-company portfolio approach. Prime Minister Mostafa Madbouly announced that a detailed executive program will be finalized by September 30. The policy groups divestment targets into three tiers, with airports, banks, and telecoms as urgent priorities. The first SOP generated USD 5.9 billion in direct proceeds across 19 transactions. State ownership will become selective, limited to cases with sovereign, strategic, or social justification, with the Sovereign Fund of Egypt managing eligible assets.

Executive Summary
The US Senate Foreign Relations Committee has approved a bipartisan bill that identifies Egypt's energy infrastructure, including the planned Greece-Egypt electricity interconnection and LNG facilities, as key links in the India-Middle East-Europe Economic Corridor. The Eastern Mediterranean Gateway Act positions Egypt, Greece, Cyprus, and Israel as a strategic gateway and seeks to institutionalize US support for greater connectivity across the region. The bill frames the corridor as a strategic alternative to Chinese-backed infrastructure initiatives. The proposed bill requires approval from the full Senate and House before becoming law. The initiative aims to enhance regional connectivity, with Egypt's LNG facilities and the Gregy interconnection project playing a crucial role. The bill offers political backing but does not provide funding. The US Senate's move raises the strategic profile of Egypt's energy infrastructure, particularly the Gregy project, within Washington's vision for the corridor.
