Executive Summary
Egypt's consumer finance sector has experienced rapid growth, sparking concerns over asset quality and borrower solvency. Former chairman of the Financial Regulatory Authority, Sherif Samy, and financial analyst Ahmed Ezz El-din, discussed the sector's structural risks and a major 'blind spot' in the nation's credit registries. The non-banking sector is broad, with its largest figures going to corporations via leasing and factoring, while consumer finance is strictly limited to installment sales to individuals. The market is heavily concentrated, with the top two banks controlling 40% of activity and the top four controlling 60%. A single default could trigger a domino effect, but the ecosystem is protected by self-regulatory mechanics. The 'blind spot' in Egypt's credit registry is caused by unrecorded long-term real estate installments, which fuels over-indebtedness. To close this loophole, special legislation is needed to integrate real estate into the national credit framework, making it mandatory for real estate companies to register all installments and financial obligations exceeding one year into the iScore database.
Future Implications
The growth of Egypt's consumer finance sector and the 'blind spot' in the credit registry may have significant implications for the real estate market and the broader financial system. If left unaddressed, the 'blind spot' could lead to over-indebtedness and increased risk of default, which could have a ripple effect on the entire financial system. On the other hand, addressing the 'blind spot' through legislation and regulation could lead to increased transparency and stability in the market, making it more attractive to investors and developers. The Egyptian government has finalized the second iteration of its State Ownership Policy, updating the 2022 framework to adopt a company-by-company portfolio approach. Prime Minister Mostafa Madbouly announced that a detailed executive program will be finalized by September 30. The policy groups divestment targets into three tiers, with airports, banks, and telecoms as urgent priorities. The first SOP generated USD 5.9 billion in direct proceeds across 19 transactions. State ownership will become selective, limited to cases with sovereign, strategic, or social justification, with the Sovereign Fund of Egypt managing eligible assets.

Executive Summary
The US Senate Foreign Relations Committee has approved a bipartisan bill that identifies Egypt's energy infrastructure, including the planned Greece-Egypt electricity interconnection and LNG facilities, as key links in the India-Middle East-Europe Economic Corridor. The Eastern Mediterranean Gateway Act positions Egypt, Greece, Cyprus, and Israel as a strategic gateway and seeks to institutionalize US support for greater connectivity across the region. The bill frames the corridor as a strategic alternative to Chinese-backed infrastructure initiatives. The proposed bill requires approval from the full Senate and House before becoming law. The initiative aims to enhance regional connectivity, with Egypt's LNG facilities and the Gregy interconnection project playing a crucial role. The bill offers political backing but does not provide funding. The US Senate's move raises the strategic profile of Egypt's energy infrastructure, particularly the Gregy project, within Washington's vision for the corridor.
